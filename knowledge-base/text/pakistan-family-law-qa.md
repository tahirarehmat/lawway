# Pakistan Family Law — Q&A

Family law in Pakistan is governed primarily by the Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 (MFLO), the Family Courts Act 1964, the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939, the Guardians and Wards Act 1890, and the Child Marriage Restraint Act 1929.

## 1. Nikah and Marriage

**Q: What makes a Nikah legally valid in Pakistan?**
**A:** For a Muslim Nikah to be legally valid, four conditions must be met. First, both parties must give free and voluntary consent. Second, there must be an offer (Ijab) and acceptance (Qabool). Third, there must be at least two adult Muslim witnesses. Fourth, a Mahr (dower) must be agreed upon. The Nikah must also be registered with the Union Council through a licensed Nikah Registrar. If it is not registered, it is still valid as a marriage but the parties can face penalties for non-registration.
**Applicable Law:** Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961, Section 5 — compulsory registration. Islamic law — conditions of valid Nikah.

**Q: Is a marriage without my free consent legally valid in Pakistan?**
**A:** No. Free and voluntary consent of both parties is a fundamental requirement for a valid Nikah under Pakistani law. A marriage contracted without the free consent of the bride or groom — for example through coercion, force, or deception — can be challenged and may be voidable or void depending on the circumstances. You can seek dissolution through the Family Court under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939 on grounds including cruelty or that the marriage was contracted under compulsion. If you were married before age 16 by a guardian, you may also have the right to repudiate the marriage before age 18 under DMMA 1939.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 5 — valid Nikah requires free consent. DMMA 1939, Section 2 — grounds for dissolution. Islamic law — consent (Rida) is essential.

**Q: My Nikah is registered in the village but I live in the city — is it still valid everywhere in Pakistan?**
**A:** Yes. A Nikah registered anywhere in Pakistan is valid throughout the country. The Nikahnama is an official legal document recognized by all courts, banks, and government offices across Pakistan regardless of where it was registered. If you need a certified copy, you can get it from the Union Council or NADRA.
**Applicable Law:** Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961, Section 5. NADRA — registration and records.

**Q: Can a woman include conditions in the Nikahnama at the time of marriage?**
**A:** Yes. Pakistani law allows a woman to include specific conditions in the Nikahnama that are binding on the husband. Common conditions include: the husband's agreement not to take a second wife without her consent; the wife's right to divorce herself (Talaq-i-Tafweez) if certain conditions arise; a higher Mahr; or the right to continue working or studying. These conditions are legally enforceable in Pakistani courts. Any woman should read the Nikahnama carefully and add her conditions before signing.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961 — conditions in Nikahnama are legally binding. Family Courts Act 1964.

**Q: I did a court marriage — is it recognized by my family, NADRA and government offices?**
**A:** A court marriage in Pakistan is a Nikah performed before a Nikah Registrar or through a Family Court, fully complying with MFLO 1961. It is 100 percent legally valid. NADRA recognizes it for issuing family registration certificates and updating CNICs. Government offices, banks, and embassies accept it. The only practical issue is social acceptance, which is a personal matter. Legally, your rights as a wife are identical to any other married woman.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 5. NADRA Act 2000.

## 2. Divorce and Dissolution

**Q: My husband gave me verbal Talaq three times — am I legally divorced?**
**A:** No. Under Pakistani law, a verbal Talaq — even if said three times — is NOT legally effective on its own. For Talaq to be valid, the husband must send a written notice to the Chairman of the Union Council. The Chairman then sends a copy to the wife. A 90-day reconciliation period then begins. Only after 90 days, if no reconciliation happens, does the Talaq become final and effective. Until that procedure is followed, you are legally still married and entitled to all your rights.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 7 — mandatory written notice to Union Council for Talaq.

**Q: My husband said Talaq twice and took me back both times. Now he said it a third time. What happens?**
**A:** Under Islamic law applicable in Pakistan, the first two Talaqs are revocable within the Iddat period. The husband can take the wife back without a new Nikah. The third Talaq is considered final and the marriage is dissolved. However, even the third Talaq must follow the MFLO 1961 procedure of written notice to the Union Council and 90-day cooling off period. If the husband pronounced three Talaqs in one sitting, Pakistani courts generally treat it as one revocable Talaq, not three. You should consult a lawyer to determine the legal status of your marriage based on the specific facts.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 7. Islamic jurisprudence — Hanafi position on triple Talaq.

**Q: What is Khula and how is it different from divorce?**
**A:** Khula is a woman's right to seek dissolution of the marriage through the court, usually by returning her Mahr (dower) to the husband. It is different from Talaq, which is the husband's right to divorce. Khula does not require the husband's consent — the wife files a suit in Family Court, and the court grants Khula even if the husband refuses. Pakistani courts also allow no-fault Khula, meaning a woman can get Khula without proving any wrongdoing by the husband, provided she is willing to return the Mahr. If the husband was at fault (for example, cruelty or abandonment), courts often allow Khula without requiring the wife to return the Mahr.
**Applicable Law:** Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939, Section 2. Family Courts Act 1964. Balqis Fatima v. Najam-ul-Ikram Chaudhry PLD 1959 Lahore 566.

**Q: On what grounds can a wife file for dissolution of marriage in Pakistan?**
**A:** Under the Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939, a wife can seek dissolution on ten grounds. One, the husband's whereabouts have been unknown for four or more years. Two, the husband has failed to maintain the wife for two or more years. Three, the husband has been sentenced to imprisonment for seven or more years. Four, the husband has failed to perform marital obligations for three years. Five, the husband was impotent at the time of marriage and remains so. Six, the husband has been insane for two years or has leprosy or a serious venereal disease. Seven, the wife was given in marriage by her guardian before age 16 and she repudiates the marriage before age 18. Eight, the husband treats her with cruelty. Nine, the husband married a second wife in violation of MFLO 1961. Ten, any other ground recognised under Islamic law.
**Applicable Law:** Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act 1939, Section 2(i) to (ix).

**Q: My husband lives abroad and I cannot contact him — can I still get a divorce?**
**A:** Yes. If your husband has been absent for four or more years and his whereabouts are unknown, you can file a dissolution suit in Family Court based on that ground alone. If he has failed to maintain you for two or more years, you can file on that ground. The court will issue notice to the husband. If he cannot be found, the court will order publication of the notice in a newspaper. After the notice period, the court can proceed ex-parte (in his absence) and grant the dissolution decree. You do not need the husband's presence or consent.
**Applicable Law:** DMMA 1939, Section 2(i) and 2(ii). Family Courts Act 1964. CPC — service by publication.

**Q: What is the Iddat period and what are my rights during it?**
**A:** Iddat is the mandatory waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or the death of her husband before she can remarry. After Talaq, the Iddat is three menstrual cycles, or three months if she does not menstruate. If she is pregnant, the Iddat continues until she delivers the child. After the death of the husband, the Iddat is four months and ten days. During the Iddat after divorce, the husband must continue to provide maintenance (food, clothing, and shelter) for the wife. The wife must also stay within the matrimonial home during Iddat unless the husband has vacated or it is unsafe.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 7. Islamic law — Iddat rules applicable through Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962.

## 3. Maintenance

**Q: My husband refuses to give me any money for household expenses — what can I do?**
**A:** You can file a maintenance application in the Family Court of your district. You do not need to file for divorce to claim maintenance. The court can issue an interim maintenance order very quickly, sometimes within the first hearing. Maintenance (Nafaqah) includes food, clothing, and shelter. The amount is determined based on the husband's income and financial capacity. Once a maintenance decree is issued, it is enforced as arrears of land revenue, meaning the court can attach the husband's salary, bank account, or property.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 9. Family Courts Act 1964. Maintenance Ordinance 1982.

**Q: After my divorce, is my ex-husband required to pay for our children's expenses?**
**A:** Yes. A father is legally obligated to maintain his minor children regardless of whether he is married to their mother or not. This obligation continues until the sons can support themselves (generally until adulthood) and until daughters are married. The amount is determined by the Family Court based on the father's income. You can file a maintenance application for the children in Family Court and the court will fix a monthly amount. Non-payment is enforced the same way as any maintenance decree.
**Applicable Law:** Family Courts Act 1964, Schedule. Islamic law — father's obligation of maintenance for children.

**Q: My father passed away — who is responsible for my maintenance as an adult unmarried daughter?**
**A:** If your father has passed away, the obligation of maintaining an unmarried adult daughter shifts to her brothers (or other male mahram relatives), provided they have the means to do so. This is based on Islamic law of maintenance. If there are no male relatives who can support her, or if they refuse, she can approach a court. In practice, Pakistani courts have ordered brothers to maintain unmarried sisters who have no income of their own.
**Applicable Law:** Islamic law of maintenance — applied through Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962.

## 4. Child Custody

**Q: My wife has left and taken our children with her — what right do I have to see them?**
**A:** As the father, you are the natural guardian of your minor children under Pakistani law. Even if the mother has physical custody (hizanat), you have the right to regular access and visitation. You can file an application in Family Court for a visitation schedule. The court will order defined dates and times for you to meet your children. If the mother is denying access completely, you can file a contempt application, and the court can enforce the visitation order. In serious cases, you can petition for a change of custody.
**Applicable Law:** Guardians and Wards Act 1890, Section 17. Family Courts Act 1964.

**Q: My husband has remarried and his new wife is mistreating my children who live with him — can I get custody?**
**A:** Yes. The welfare of the child is the paramount consideration in all custody decisions. If you can show that the children's physical or emotional welfare is being harmed in the father's home, the court can transfer custody to you. Evidence such as medical records showing injury, school records showing neglect, or witness testimony about mistreatment can support your case. File an application for change of guardianship in Family Court immediately. The court may also order a welfare inquiry.
**Applicable Law:** Guardians and Wards Act 1890, Section 17 — welfare of minor is paramount.

**Q: My husband wants to take our children to another country for 'a visit' — should I be worried?**
**A:** Yes, you should take this seriously. If there is a custody order or a court case pending, your husband cannot take the children abroad without court permission. You can file an urgent application in Family Court to prevent departure and ask for the children's names to be placed on the Exit Control List (ECL) through the Federal Investigation Agency. If no court case is pending yet, file one immediately. Pakistan is now a signatory to the Hague Convention on International Child Abduction, so courts can order the return of children taken abroad without consent.
**Applicable Law:** Family Courts Act 1964, Section 5, Schedule 6A (added by SRO 980/2017) — Hague Convention. PPC Section 361 — kidnapping from lawful guardianship.

## 5. Inheritance

**Q: My father died without a will. How is his property divided among his children?**
**A:** Under Islamic inheritance law, which applies to all Muslims in Pakistan, the estate is divided among legal heirs according to fixed shares. If your father left a wife, sons, and daughters, the distribution is as follows. The wife receives one-eighth of the estate. The remaining seven-eighths is divided among sons and daughters in the ratio of two to one, meaning each son receives double the share of each daughter. For example, if there are two sons and two daughters, each son gets two-sevenths of the residue and each daughter gets one-seventh. There is no need to go to court if all heirs agree. If they disagree, you can file a partition suit in civil court.
**Applicable Law:** Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962. Quranic inheritance rules (Fara'id).

**Q: My brothers are saying that as a daughter I have no right to the agricultural land — is this correct?**
**A:** No. This is completely wrong. Before 1962, agricultural land in Pakistan was sometimes distributed according to customary law, which excluded daughters. The West Pakistan Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962 specifically abolished this practice and mandated that Islamic inheritance law applies to ALL property, including agricultural land. Your brothers cannot legally exclude you from your share. If they do, you can file a civil suit for your share AND a criminal complaint under PPC Section 498-A, which makes depriving a woman of her inheritance a criminal offence punishable by 5 to 10 years imprisonment and a fine of one million rupees.
**Applicable Law:** Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962. PPC 1860, Section 498-A — deprivation of inheritance.

**Q: My husband passed away and my in-laws are saying his property belongs to them — what are my rights?**
**A:** As the wife of the deceased, you are a legal heir. If your husband had no children, you are entitled to one-fourth of his estate. If he had children, you are entitled to one-eighth. The in-laws (parents) are also heirs, but they cannot take your share. Your share must be given to you before any other distributions. If your in-laws are refusing or hiding property, you can file a suit for your inheritance share in civil court. Also file a revenue mutation application at the local Patwari office to get your name recorded in the property record.
**Applicable Law:** Islamic inheritance rules — wife's share is fixed. Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962. PPC Section 498-A.

**Q: My son died before my husband. Do my grandchildren (my son's children) inherit from my husband?**
**A:** Under classical Islamic law, grandchildren whose parent has died before the grandparent do not inherit if there are surviving uncles or aunts. However, Pakistan has reformed this by law. Section 4 of the Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 provides that if a son or daughter dies before the grandparent, the grandchildren receive the share that their parent would have received, per stirpes. This is a significant protection for orphaned grandchildren. This rule applies regardless of whether the grandchildren are boys or girls.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 4 — inheritance of orphaned grandchildren. Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962.

## 6. Dowry and Mahr

**Q: My in-laws are demanding more dowry after marriage and threatening me — is this legal?**
**A:** No. Demanding dowry is a criminal offence in Pakistan. The Dowry and Bridal Gifts (Restriction) Act 1976 prohibits demanding or giving dowry as a condition of marriage. You can file a complaint at the police station under this Act. Punishment is imprisonment up to six months and a fine. Additionally, if the demands are accompanied by threats or violence, you can file for criminal intimidation under PPC Section 506. The items you brought as your bridal gifts (jahez) are your personal property. If anyone takes or damages them, you can file a case for criminal breach of trust under PPC Section 406.
**Applicable Law:** Dowry and Bridal Gifts (Restriction) Act 1976. PPC Section 406 (breach of trust). PPC Section 506 (criminal intimidation).

**Q: My husband divorced me but never paid my Mahr. Can I still claim it years later?**
**A:** Yes. Your Mahr is your absolute legal right and you can claim it at any time. If it was prompt Mahr (mu'ajjal), it was payable on demand during the marriage. If it was deferred Mahr (mu'wajjal), it becomes payable on dissolution of the marriage or death of the husband. You can file a suit for recovery of Mahr in Family Court. There is no time bar that prevents you from claiming it, though the court may consider delay as a factor. The Mahr amount recorded in the Nikahnama is the legal amount.
**Applicable Law:** MFLO 1961, Section 10. Family Courts Act 1964 — dower suit in Family Court.
