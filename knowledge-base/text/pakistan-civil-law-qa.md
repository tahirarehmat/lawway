# Pakistan Civil Law — Q&A

Civil law in Pakistan covers disputes between private parties — individuals, companies, or organizations — where one party seeks compensation or a court order rather than criminal punishment. The main legislation is the Code of Civil Procedure 1908 (CPC), the Contract Act 1872, the Specific Relief Act 1877, the Transfer of Property Act 1882, and the Limitation Act 1908.

## 1. Property Disputes

**Q: Someone sold my property without my knowledge using forged documents — what can I do?**
**A:** You have both a civil remedy and a criminal remedy. Civilly, file a suit for declaration that the sale is void, cancellation of the sale deed, and recovery of possession in civil court. The court can declare the fraudulent sale null and void and restore the property to you. Criminally, file an FIR for forgery (PPC Section 467), cheating (PPC Section 420), and criminal breach of trust (PPC Section 406) against the persons who forged the documents. Also file a complaint with the local revenue authorities (Patwari and DC) to stop any further mutation of the property.
**Applicable Law:** Specific Relief Act 1877, Sections 39 and 42 — cancellation of instruments and declaratory relief. PPC Sections 420, 467.

**Q: I paid money to a builder for a flat but he has not given possession — what are my options?**
**A:** You have several options. First, send the builder a legal notice through a lawyer demanding possession within a specified time. Second, if he does not comply, file a suit for specific performance of the contract in civil court — the court can order him to hand over the flat. Third, if he has fraudulently taken money from many buyers, you can file a criminal complaint for cheating under PPC Section 420. Fourth, if the project is registered with the Real Estate Regulatory Authority (RERA), you can file a complaint there. Keep all payment receipts, agreements, and communications as evidence.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872. Specific Relief Act 1877, Section 12 — specific performance of contract. PPC Section 420.

**Q: My landlord is refusing to return my security deposit after I vacated — what can I do?**
**A:** First, send the landlord a written legal notice demanding return of the security deposit within a specified period. If he does not return it, you can file a civil suit for recovery of money. The landlord can only deduct from the security deposit the actual cost of any damage caused by you — not normal wear and tear. If he refuses to return it without valid reason, you can also argue that he has committed criminal breach of trust under PPC Section 406. Keep all evidence: the tenancy agreement, payment receipts, and a record of the condition of the property when you vacated.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872 — terms of tenancy agreement. Rent Restriction Ordinance (applicable in ICT). PPC Section 406.

**Q: My neighbour has encroached on part of my land — what legal action can I take?**
**A:** First, obtain your land record (Fard) from the local Patwari to confirm your boundaries. Then send a legal notice to the neighbour. If they do not remove the encroachment, file a civil suit for declaration of your ownership and possession, and for a permanent injunction restraining further encroachment. You can also apply for an interim injunction at the start of the case to prevent further construction. Additionally, you can file an application before the local revenue authorities for resolution of boundary disputes, which can be faster than a full civil suit.
**Applicable Law:** Specific Relief Act 1877 — injunction and declaration. Transfer of Property Act 1882. Land Revenue Act 1967.

**Q: I bought a car and paid in full but the seller is not transferring the registration — what should I do?**
**A:** Send a legal notice to the seller immediately. If he does not comply, file a civil suit for specific performance of the agreement to sell, and also approach the Excise and Taxation Department with your sale agreement to process the transfer. If the seller is deliberately withholding transfer to sell the car again to someone else, this may also be fraud — you can file an FIR for cheating under PPC Section 420. Keep your original sale agreement and all payment evidence safe.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872. Specific Relief Act 1877. PPC Section 420.

## 2. Contracts and Money Recovery

**Q: I lent money to someone and they are refusing to return it — how do I recover it?**
**A:** You can file a civil suit for recovery of money in the civil court of the area where the borrower lives or where the contract was made. The court will summon the defendant, hear the case, and if satisfied, issue a money decree. Once you have a decree, it can be executed by attaching the debtor's property, salary, or bank account. If you have a written agreement, cheque, or promissory note, recovery is easier. Even without a written agreement, you can prove the loan through witnesses and circumstantial evidence. The Limitation Act gives you three years from the date the money was due to file the suit.
**Applicable Law:** Civil Procedure Code 1908. Contract Act 1872. Limitation Act 1908, Article 64 — three-year limitation for money recovery.

**Q: A company has not paid my invoices for services I performed — can I take legal action?**
**A:** Yes. Send a formal legal notice to the company first, giving them a specific deadline to pay. If they do not pay, file a suit for recovery of money in civil court. For amounts above a certain threshold, you can also consider filing in a Commercial Court, which handles business disputes faster. Keep all invoices, delivery receipts, emails, and any written acknowledgment of the debt. If the company disputes the amount owed, you may have to prove completion of the service through evidence.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872. Civil Procedure Code 1908. Commercial Courts Act 2015 (for commercial disputes).

**Q: Someone has given me a cheque as payment but it bounced — what are all my options?**
**A:** You have two parallel remedies. Criminally, file an FIR at the police station for dishonestly issuing a cheque under PPC Section 489-F — this is a cognizable offence and the accused can be arrested. The punishment is imprisonment up to three years and a fine. Civilly, file a suit for recovery of the cheque amount in civil court. Keep the original dishonoured cheque and the bank's return memo — these are your primary evidence. Send a legal notice to the drawer of the cheque before filing court cases, giving them a chance to pay.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 489-F. Negotiable Instruments Act 1881. Civil Procedure Code 1908.

**Q: I signed a contract under pressure — can I get out of it?**
**A:** Yes. A contract signed under coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation is voidable at the option of the party who was pressured. You can file a suit to have the contract declared void or you can simply inform the other party that you are rescinding (cancelling) the contract. You will generally need to return any benefit you received under the contract. Act quickly — delay may make it harder to prove that the contract was signed under pressure. Collect any evidence of the threat, coercion, or fraud that was used against you.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872, Sections 14 to 19 — consent obtained by coercion, undue influence, fraud, or misrepresentation makes a contract voidable.

## 3. Consumer Rights

**Q: I bought a product that is defective and the seller is refusing to replace or refund — what can I do?**
**A:** You can file a complaint under the Consumer Protection Act. Most provinces have consumer protection tribunals or courts. In Punjab, the Punjab Consumer Protection Act 2005 applies. In Sindh, the Sindh Consumer Protection Act 2014 applies. At the federal level, the Competition Act and Consumer Protection Act 2019 apply. File a complaint with the relevant consumer protection authority. You can seek replacement of the defective product, a full refund, and compensation for any harm suffered. The process is generally faster and cheaper than filing a full civil suit.
**Applicable Law:** Consumer Protection Act 2019 (federal). Punjab Consumer Protection Act 2005. Sindh Consumer Protection Act 2014.

**Q: A hospital or doctor gave me wrong treatment — can I sue for medical negligence?**
**A:** Yes. Medical negligence is both a civil wrong (tort) and potentially a criminal offence if it causes death (PPC Section 322 — Qatl-bis-sabab). For a civil claim, you file a suit for damages against the doctor and hospital in civil court, arguing that they failed to meet the standard of care expected of a competent doctor. You need expert medical evidence to support your case. The PMDC (Pakistan Medical and Dental Council) also has a disciplinary process for complaints against doctors. Get all medical records and reports before they are altered or lost.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 322. Civil law of tort — negligence. Pakistan Medical and Dental Council Ordinance 1962.

## 4. Defamation

**Q: Someone made false and damaging statements about me publicly — can I sue them?**
**A:** Yes. Defamation in Pakistan is both a criminal offence and a civil wrong. A defamatory statement is one that is false, made to a third party, and damages your reputation. For a criminal case, file an FIR under PPC Section 499 and 500. The punishment is imprisonment up to two years. For a civil case, file a suit for damages in civil court. You can claim compensation for financial loss, emotional distress, and harm to your reputation. Truth is a complete defence to defamation — the defendant can escape liability by proving the statement was true.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 499 (defamation defined), Section 500 (punishment — up to 2 years). Civil tort law.

## 5. Labour and Employment

**Q: My employer fired me without notice or reason — what are my rights?**
**A:** An employer cannot terminate a permanent employee without following due process. You are entitled to: one month's notice or one month's salary in lieu of notice; gratuity based on your length of service (usually one month's salary per year of service); and any outstanding wages, overtime, and leave encashment. If the termination was without cause and without following due process, it may be wrongful termination. You can file a complaint with the Labour Court of your province. The Labour Court can order reinstatement or payment of compensation. You must file within the limitation period, which varies by province.
**Applicable Law:** Industrial and Commercial Employment (Standing Orders) Ordinance 1968. Industrial Relations Act 2012. West Pakistan Shops and Establishments Ordinance 1969.

**Q: My employer has not paid my salary for three months — what can I do?**
**A:** Non-payment of wages is a criminal offence in Pakistan. You can file a complaint under the Payment of Wages Act 1936 before the Payment of Wages Authority or Labour Court. The employer can be fined and ordered to pay your outstanding wages with a penalty. You can also file a civil suit for recovery of wages. Additionally, you can contact the Regional Labour Office of your province to lodge a complaint. Keep all evidence of employment: offer letter, salary slips, bank records, and any communication about unpaid salary.
**Applicable Law:** Payment of Wages Act 1936. Industrial Relations Act 2012. West Pakistan Shops and Establishments Ordinance 1969.

**Q: Can my employer reduce my salary without my consent?**
**A:** No. Your salary is a contractual term of your employment. An employer cannot unilaterally reduce your salary without your consent. If they do, it is a breach of the employment contract. You can refuse to accept the reduction and continue working at your original salary, or file a complaint in Labour Court. If you were forced to sign a new contract with a lower salary under pressure, that agreement may be voidable under the Contract Act 1872 as signed under coercion or undue influence.
**Applicable Law:** Contract Act 1872. Industrial and Commercial Employment (Standing Orders) Ordinance 1968.

## 6. General Civil Procedure

**Q: What is a legal notice and why do I need to send one before filing a case?**
**A:** A legal notice is a formal written communication from a lawyer informing the other party of your legal claim and giving them an opportunity to settle before court proceedings begin. For many types of civil cases, sending a legal notice is a legal requirement before filing a suit. Even where it is not mandatory, courts expect you to have tried to resolve the matter first. A legal notice also serves as evidence that you tried to resolve the dispute amicably. It must be sent by registered post or through a lawyer, and you should keep the acknowledgment receipt.
**Applicable Law:** Specific Relief Act 1877, Section 80 — notice required before suit against government. General practice — legal notice recommended before all civil suits.

**Q: What is the limitation period — how long do I have to file a civil case?**
**A:** The Limitation Act 1908 sets time limits for filing different types of civil suits. If you miss the limitation period, your case will be dismissed regardless of its merits. Common limitation periods are as follows. For recovery of money based on a contract, the limit is three years. For recovery of property, it is twelve years. For defamation, it is one year. For cases against the government, it is sometimes as short as one year. Limitation begins to run from the date the cause of action arises, which is usually the date of the breach or the date you first suffered the harm. See a lawyer immediately if you think your case may be close to the limitation period.
**Applicable Law:** Limitation Act 1908 — detailed schedule of limitation periods for different suits.

**Q: What is an injunction and when can I get one from the court?**
**A:** An injunction is a court order that directs a party to do something or stop doing something. A temporary injunction (interim stay) can be obtained at the very beginning of a case, even on the first hearing, if you can show that you will suffer irreparable harm without it, that you have a good case on merit, and that the balance of convenience favours granting the injunction. A permanent injunction is granted after the full hearing of the case. Injunctions are commonly used to stop construction on disputed land, prevent sale of property during a case, or stop publication of defamatory content.
**Applicable Law:** Specific Relief Act 1877, Sections 53 to 55 — injunctions. Civil Procedure Code 1908, Order XXXIX — temporary injunctions.

**Q: I want to file a case against the government — is that possible and how do I do it?**
**A:** Yes, you can file cases against the government. For civil matters, you generally need to send a notice to the government department two months before filing the suit. For constitutional matters — such as violations of fundamental rights — you can file a writ petition directly in the High Court without the two-month notice. For service-related matters (government employees), you go to the Service Tribunal. For matters involving illegal actions by government officials, you can seek a writ of certiorari or mandamus from the High Court to quash the illegal action or compel the official to perform their duty.
**Applicable Law:** Specific Relief Act 1877, Section 80 — prior notice required before suit against government. Constitution of Pakistan, Article 199 — High Court writ jurisdiction.
