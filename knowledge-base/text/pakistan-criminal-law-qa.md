# Pakistan Criminal Law — Q&A

## A. FIR and Police Procedures

**Q: What is an FIR and do I have a right to file one?**
**A:** FIR stands for First Information Report. It is the first written document recording a criminal complaint at a police station. Every person who has knowledge of a cognizable offence has the right to report it to the nearest police station. The police are legally required to register the FIR — they cannot refuse. If they refuse, you can approach the Superintendent of Police or a Magistrate who can order the registration.
**Applicable Law:** Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, Section 154 — registration of FIR is mandatory for cognizable offences.

**Q: What is the difference between a cognizable and non-cognizable offence?**
**A:** A cognizable offence is one where the police can arrest a person without a warrant and can investigate without the Magistrate's prior permission. Examples include murder, robbery, rape, and kidnapping. A non-cognizable offence requires police to get a Magistrate's permission before arresting or investigating. Examples include defamation, cheating of a minor nature, and simple assault.
**Applicable Law:** CrPC 1898, First Schedule — list of cognizable and non-cognizable offences.

**Q: What can I do if the police refuse to register my FIR?**
**A:** If police refuse to register your FIR, you have three options. First, you can file a written complaint with the Superintendent of Police (SP) or Deputy Inspector General (DIG) of your area. Second, you can file a complaint directly before a Judicial Magistrate under Section 22-A CrPC, who can direct the police to register the FIR. Third, you can file a writ petition in the High Court. In practice, going to the Magistrate under Section 22-A is the fastest remedy.
**Applicable Law:** CrPC 1898, Section 22-A — Magistrate can direct police to register FIR.

**Q: Can I register an FIR against an unknown person?**
**A:** Yes. You can file an FIR against an unknown person or persons. You describe the offence, the time, the place, and whatever description you know of the person. The police will then investigate and identify the accused. This is common in robbery, theft, and acid attack cases where the victim does not know the attacker.
**Applicable Law:** CrPC 1898, Section 154.

## B. Arrest and Bail

**Q: Can the police arrest me without a warrant?**
**A:** In a cognizable offence, yes — the police can arrest you without a warrant. However, even then they must have a reasonable basis. For non-cognizable offences, they need a Magistrate's warrant. After arrest, you must be produced before a Magistrate within 24 hours. The police cannot keep you in custody longer than 24 hours without a Magistrate's remand order.
**Applicable Law:** Constitution of Pakistan, Article 10 — rights of arrested person. CrPC 1898, Section 61.

**Q: What is bail and what are my rights to get bail?**
**A:** Bail is the temporary release of an accused person from custody, usually on condition that they appear in court when required. Offences are classified as bailable and non-bailable. For bailable offences, bail is a right — the police or court must grant it. For non-bailable offences (such as murder), bail is discretionary and only a court can grant it. You can apply for bail before the Sessions Court or High Court.
**Applicable Law:** CrPC 1898, Sections 496 to 502 — bail provisions. PPC 1860 — classification of offences.

**Q: What is pre-arrest bail (anticipatory bail) and how do I get it?**
**A:** Pre-arrest bail is a court order obtained before you are arrested. If you fear you may be falsely arrested, you can apply to the Sessions Court or High Court for pre-arrest bail. The court will hear your application and, if satisfied that the arrest would be unjust or the case is false, may grant you bail before any arrest happens. This protects you from being taken into custody while the case proceeds.
**Applicable Law:** CrPC 1898, Section 498 — High Court or Sessions Court can grant bail at any stage.

**Q: My family member has been arrested — what should I do in the first 24 hours?**
**A:** First, find out which police station they are held at. Second, immediately engage a lawyer to file a bail application. Third, the police must produce the arrested person before a Magistrate within 24 hours of arrest — attend that hearing with a lawyer. Fourth, you can apply for physical remand extension to be denied and for your family member to be sent to judicial custody instead of police custody. Fifth, if the police deny access to the family, file a complaint with the SP or directly approach the High Court with a habeas corpus petition.
**Applicable Law:** Constitution, Article 10. CrPC 1898, Section 61.

## 2. Murder, Hurt and Bodily Harm

**Q: What is the difference between murder (Qatl-e-Amd) and culpable homicide under Pakistani law?**
**A:** Qatl-e-Amd (intentional murder) under the PPC is the deliberate killing of a person with the intention to kill or with knowledge that death is likely to result. It is punishable by death (Qisas) or imprisonment and Diyat (blood money) if the heirs pardon the killer. Culpable homicide not amounting to murder is when death is caused but without the full intention required for murder — it is punishable by imprisonment up to 25 years. The key difference is the degree of intention.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Sections 300 (Qatl-e-Amd), 302 (punishment), and 316 (culpable homicide).

**Q: What is Qisas and Diyat — can a murderer be pardoned by the victim's family?**
**A:** Qisas means equal retaliation — the punishment is the same as the crime (death for death). Under Pakistani law, the legal heirs of the murder victim have the right to either demand Qisas (execution of the killer), accept Diyat (blood money as compensation), or pardon the killer entirely. If the heirs accept Diyat or pardon, the state can still punish the killer under Ta'zir (discretionary punishment) with imprisonment up to 25 years. The state cannot override a pardon by the heirs in a Qisas case.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Sections 299 to 338-H — Qisas and Diyat provisions.

**Q: I was attacked and seriously injured — what case can I file and what punishment does the attacker face?**
**A:** You can file an FIR for 'hurt' under the PPC. The severity of the charge depends on the injury. Simple hurt carries imprisonment up to one year. Grievous hurt (such as fracture, permanent disfigurement, or loss of an organ) carries imprisonment up to seven years. If the injury was caused with a weapon, the punishment is higher. Additionally, you can claim Arsh (compensation for injuries that have a fixed value in Islamic law) or Daman (compensation for injuries without a fixed value). File the FIR and get a Medico-Legal Certificate (MLC) from a government hospital immediately.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Sections 332 to 338 — hurt, grievous hurt, Arsh, and Daman.

**Q: What is the punishment for acid attacks in Pakistan?**
**A:** Acid attacks are a specific offence under the PPC. If the acid attack causes permanent disfigurement of any part of the body, the punishment is either: Qisas (the same injury inflicted on the attacker, which courts rarely apply), or imprisonment for a minimum of 14 years up to life imprisonment. The victim is also entitled to Daman (compensation) for pain, suffering, and medical expenses. The court determines the amount of Daman based on the severity of the injury.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Sections 336-A and 336-B — acid attacks, added by Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2011.

## 3. Theft, Robbery and Property Offences

**Q: What is the difference between theft and robbery under Pakistani law?**
**A:** Theft is taking someone's property dishonestly, without their consent, and without using force or fear. Robbery is theft that involves force or the threat of force against the person. If robbery is committed by five or more persons together, it becomes dacoity, which carries a much heavier punishment. Theft is punishable by imprisonment up to three years and fine. Robbery is punishable by up to ten years imprisonment. Dacoity carries imprisonment up to ten years, and if injury or murder is involved, up to life imprisonment or death.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 378 (theft), Section 390 (robbery), Section 391 (dacoity).

**Q: Someone has illegally occupied my property — what criminal case can I file?**
**A:** You can file a case under the Illegal Dispossession Act 2005, which is a criminal law specifically for illegal occupation of immoveable property. Under this Act, if someone dispossesses you of property by force, fraud, or any other illegal means, you can file a complaint before a Magistrate. The Magistrate can restore possession within 30 days and punish the offender with imprisonment up to three years and a fine. This is faster than a civil suit for possession.
**Applicable Law:** Illegal Dispossession Act 2005, Section 3. Also CrPC Section 145 for immediate possession orders.

**Q: What is cheating under Pakistani law and can I file an FIR for it?**
**A:** Cheating means deceiving a person dishonestly to deliver property or money, or to do or not do something which causes harm. For example, taking money with a false promise to supply goods or perform a service, and then not doing it. Cheating is a cognizable offence and you can file an FIR. The punishment is imprisonment up to one year for simple cheating. If the cheating involves impersonation or false documents, the punishment goes up to seven years.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 415 (cheating), Section 417 (punishment for cheating), Section 419 (cheating by impersonation).

**Q: Someone wrote a cheque that bounced — is that a criminal offence?**
**A:** Yes. Issuing a cheque knowing that there are insufficient funds, or dishonestly issuing a cheque, is a criminal offence of cheating and dishonest inducement. You can file an FIR under PPC Section 489-F specifically for dishonoured cheques. The punishment is imprisonment up to three years and a fine. Additionally, you can file a civil suit to recover the money. Always demand the original bounced cheque from your bank before filing any case.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 489-F — dishonestly issuing a cheque. Negotiable Instruments Act 1881.

## 4. Fraud, Forgery and Corruption

**Q: What is forgery and what is the punishment under Pakistani law?**
**A:** Forgery means making a false document or part of a document with the intent to cause damage or injury, to support a fraudulent claim, or to commit fraud. Examples include forging a signature on a property document, a Nikahnama, a will, or a contract. The punishment for simple forgery is imprisonment up to two years. If the forged document is a valuable security (like a cheque, property deed, or court document), the punishment goes up to seven years imprisonment and a fine.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 463 (forgery), Section 467 (forgery of valuable security), Section 468 (forgery for purpose of cheating).

**Q: A government official is demanding a bribe from me — what can I do?**
**A:** Demanding or accepting a bribe by a public servant is a serious criminal offence. You can approach the National Accountability Bureau (NAB), the Federal Investigation Agency (FIA), or file a complaint with the Anti-Corruption Establishment of your province. You can also use the free number to report corruption. The accused can be prosecuted under NAO 1999 or Prevention of Corruption Act 1947. Punishment includes imprisonment, confiscation of assets, and disqualification from public office.
**Applicable Law:** National Accountability Ordinance 1999. Prevention of Corruption Act 1947. PPC Section 161 (public servant taking bribe).

## 5. Cybercrime

**Q: Someone has made a fake social media profile in my name and is defaming me — what can I do?**
**A:** Creating a fake identity online, sending harassing messages, and online defamation are all criminal offences under the Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act 2016 (PECA). You can file a complaint with the Federal Investigation Agency (FIA) Cybercrime Wing. The FIA has offices in all major cities. You can also file an online complaint at fia.gov.pk. Punishment for identity spoofing and online harassment is imprisonment up to three years and a fine up to one million rupees.
**Applicable Law:** Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act 2016, Sections 16 (identity fraud), 20 (cyber harassment), 21 (cyberstalking).

**Q: Someone is blackmailing me using my private photos or videos — is this a crime?**
**A:** Yes. Sharing or threatening to share private, intimate, or sexual images or videos without consent is a specific criminal offence under PECA 2016. The punishment is imprisonment up to five years and a fine up to five million rupees. If the person is blackmailing you but has not yet shared the content, report it immediately to the FIA Cybercrime Wing. They can act quickly to prevent sharing and arrest the person. Do not pay any money to the blackmailer — it will only encourage further demands.
**Applicable Law:** PECA 2016, Section 21 (cyberstalking) and Section 20 (cyber harassment). PPC Section 507 (criminal intimidation by anonymous communication).

**Q: Someone is spreading false information about me online — can I sue them?**
**A:** Yes. Online defamation is both a criminal offence and a civil wrong in Pakistan. Criminally, you can file an FIR under PPC Section 499 (defamation) and Section 500 (punishment for defamation — imprisonment up to two years). Under PECA 2016, online defamation carries additional penalties. Civilly, you can file a suit for damages for defamation in a civil court and claim compensation. Collect screenshots of all the defamatory content before filing any case.
**Applicable Law:** PPC 1860, Section 499 and 500 (defamation). PECA 2016, Section 20 (cyber harassment).
